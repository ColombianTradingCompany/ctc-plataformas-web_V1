How these colors are determined

The genuine Agtron/SCA roast color disks are physical, individually calibrated tiles — Agtron Inc. and the SCA have never published an official CMYK or RGB formula for them. This tool builds an approximation instead, so it can be used without a $300+ color-disk kit or a spectrophotometer.

Eight reference points (Agtron #25, #35, #45, #55, #65, #75, #85, #95 — the numbers used on the physical SCA disk set) were assigned CIE L*a*b* values, informed by published Agtron-to-L*a*b* correlations from roast color research and color-selector comparison charts. Values for every other dial position are produced by interpolating between those anchors in L*a*b* space, then converting to sRGB. The Commercial scale is estimated by rescaling the Gourmet curve to its roughly 0–75 usable range (Commercial ≈ Gourmet × 0.75), since Agtron has not published a direct formula between the two.

The dial alone traces one lightness-driven path through color space, but real beans of the same roast level can still look more reddish, more ashy/gray, lighter, or darker depending on origin, variety, drying, or lighting when photographed. The Tint slider nudges the color sideways along the red↔green axis (CIE a*), and the Brightness stepper shifts lightness (CIE L*) — both independently of the dial. When you use Pick a photo, the shutter searches the dial position, tint, and brightness together to find the closest overall match to your sampled pixels.

The RGB figure shown is the exact sRGB channel values used to render this color on screen — not a conversion or approximation, unlike a CMYK print value would require.

For a more accurate comparison

That bright spot on a whole bean isn't the roast color — it's a reflection of whatever light is above you.

  • Grind, don't compare whole beans. This is why the real SCA/Agtron kit is always read on ground coffee, not whole beans — a curved, oily surface will glare at some angle no matter what. Ground coffee is matte and reads true.
  • If comparing whole beans: tilt the bean about 30–45° so the hot spot slides off, and judge color from the shaded flank — not the glare, and not the dark shadow edge.
  • Use diffuse light. A single bright point (window, lamp, a very bright screen) is what causes the glare. Cloudy daylight or a bounced lamp gives a far more even read; try dimming your screen a notch too.

Most reliable: don't eyeball it. Use the shutter button on your uploaded photo to sample real pixels instead of judging by eye.
